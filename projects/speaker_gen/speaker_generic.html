Background

Generic statements‚Äîthose about categories rather than individuals (e.g., ‚ÄúBirds fly,‚Äù ‚ÄúGirls wear pink‚Äù)‚Äîcan subtly shape beliefs, leading listeners to view categories as having fixed, intrinsic properties. The effect of these statements depends on pragmatic cues, which are signals in communication‚Äîlike the speaker‚Äôs perceived expertise‚Äîthat help listeners interpret meaning beyond the literal words. In this study, I investigated whether speaker knowledge influences how children and adults interpret generic (versus specific) statements about a novel social category called Zarpies.

Participants

  • Children: 233 children (ages 5‚Äì10; 54% girls) recruited online using PANDA.

  • Adults: 217 adults (ages 18‚Äì83; 49% female, 1% non-binary) recruited online using Prolific.

Procedure

Participants watched short narrated videos introducing a novel social category (‚ÄúZarpies‚Äù) with a unique property or behavior. Each statement varied in language type (generic vs. specific) and speaker knowledge (knowledgeable vs. unknowledgeable). Participants responded immediately after each statement, minimizing memory demands. Trials assessed three aspects of essentialist thinking:

  1. Category-Based Explanations ‚Äì whether behaviors were attributed to category membership or individual choice.

  2. Flexibility of Category Boundaries ‚Äì whether a member could display behaviors outside the category norm.

  3. Within-Category Homogeneity - how many members share a trait.

Modeling

The logistic mixed-effects models used to examine the effects of speaker knowledge and language on essentialist responses were specified as follows.

Adult Model

\[ \begin{aligned} \text{logit}\big(P(Y_{ij}=1)\big) =\ &\\ &\beta_0\ + \\ &\beta_1 \text{Knowledge}_i\ + \\ &\beta_2 \text{Language}_i\ + \\ &\beta_3 (\text{Knowledge}_i \times \text{Language}_i)\ + \\ &u_j + v_i \end{aligned}\] Where:
- \(Y_{ij}\) = binary response of participant (\(i\)) on item (\(j\))
- \(u_j \sim N(0, \sigma^2_{\text{item}})\) = random intercept for item
- \(v_i \sim N(0, \sigma^2_{\text{ID}})\) = random intercept for participant

model_adult <- glmer(response ~ knowledge * language + (1|item) + (1|ID),
                          data = subset(model_data, age_group == "adult"), 
                          family = binomial, 
                          glmerControl(optimizer = "bobyqa", 
                                       optCtrl=list(maxfun=2e5)))

Child Model (with centered age)

\[ \begin{aligned} \text{logit}\big(P(Y_{ij}=1)\big) =\ & \\ &\beta_0\ + \\ &\beta_1 \text{Knowledge}_i + \beta_2 \text{Language}_i\ + \\ &\beta_3 \text{Age}_i\ + \\ &\beta_4 (\text{Knowledge}_i \times \text{Language}_i)\ + \\ &\beta_5 (\text{Knowledge}_i \times \text{Age}_i)\ + \\ &\beta_6 (\text{Language}_i \times \text{Age}_i)\ + \\ &\beta_7 (\text{Knowledge}_i \times \text{Language}_i \times \text{Age}_i)\ + \\ &u_j + v_i \end{aligned}\]

Where \(\text{Age}_i\) is a centered continuous predictor.

There was a significant interaction between language and speaker knowledge (Wald \(\chi^2\)(1) = 8.94, p < 0.01), indicating that the effect of generic language depended on the speaker‚Äôs knowledge. Age group did not moderate this effect. When the speaker was knowledgeable, participants were 3.05 times more likely to endorse essentialist beliefs in the generic condition (\(\beta\) = 1.10, p < 0.0001).

Visual inspection of this figure suggested that younger children showed little differentiation between knowledgeable and unknowledgeable speakers, whereas older children appeared increasingly sensitive to speaker knowledge when interpreting generic statements. To probe this possibility, I conducted post hoc analyses examining the regions of significance for age.

Post-Hoc Analysis

Further analysis using the Johnson-Neyman technique identified that the effect of speaker knowledge became statistically significant in the generic condition around age 7 and not earlier, suggesting that older children were more likely to differentiate between knowledgeable and unknowledgeable speakers when interpreting generic statements. Given that the three-way interaction with participant age was not reliable, however, these possible age-related changes should be interpreted with caution.

Discussion

These findings suggest that generic language influences how people think about social categories. When a knowledgeable speaker uses generic statements, listeners are more likely to adopt the idea that the category is coherent and meaningful. In contrast, statements from an unknowledgeable speaker have less impact.

Children around age 7 and older were especially sensitive to speaker knowledge, indicating they are beginning to consider a speaker‚Äôs expertise when interpreting general statements. Younger children were also influenced by generic language, but their ability to track speaker knowledge may depend on factors like memory and attention.

Future research could test this directly by manipulating memory demands or using subtler cues of speaker knowledge, like prior accuracy or confidence. I also plan to investigate domains that do not rely on culturally specific knowledge‚Äîsuch as animal categories‚Äîto clarify the generalizability of these effects and further isolate the roles of speaker knowledge and language in shaping essentialist beliefs.
